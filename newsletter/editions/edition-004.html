The AI Training Went Great. Then Monday Came.

Education is the starting line. The value shows up later, in the unglamorous work of deciding where AI belongs, who can touch what data, and which pilot connects to a number that matters.

Editor's Note

Somewhere in your building, or a building a lot like yours, a team sat through an AI training this spring. People nodded. A few got genuinely excited. Someone made a slide. Then Monday came, the real work showed up, and almost nothing about how that work actually got done changed.

I see this everywhere, and it is not a failure of the people in the room, it is a misunderstanding about what a training is for. A training shows your staff what AI can do. It does not decide where AI belongs in your shop, who is allowed to put which data into it, or which pilot connects to a number your leadership actually cares about. That second list is the job, and it is the part almost everyone skips. Education is the starting line. The value shows up later, in the part that does not fit on a slide.

Main Essay

Here is the shift that makes AI useful, and it has five moving parts. AI starts paying off when leaders choose the right use cases, when data access is mapped so people know what they can and cannot feed a model, when policies are written to fit the actual work instead of a generic template, when staff understand where AI belongs and where it does not, and when every pilot is tied to a measurable operational goal. Miss those and you get what most organizations got this year: a lot of enthusiasm and a flat result.

None of that is technical work. It is judgment and design. Which is why the seat that closes the gap is not your most senior engineer, it is whoever can look at a workflow and say, this step is a good place for AI, this one is not, and here is the boundary in between.

The boundary part gets harder the more regulated you are. If you work in healthcare, or anywhere with protected data, you cannot treat this casually. You have HIPAA, business associate agreements, EHR boundaries, and audit requirements that a generic chatbot rollout ignores by default. The answer is not to avoid AI, it is to give the tools better context through approved sources and retrieval patterns, and to put guardrails around them before staff start pasting in things they should not.

And the people involved do not all need the same thing. Executives need a view of strategy, risk appetite, and where to spend first. IT and security need access controls, approved tools, and clear data boundaries. Managers need to pick the workflows and own the adoption metrics. Frontline staff need practical support for the documentation and navigation they do every day. One training pitched at all four at once lands with none of them.

Use Case

What we built with Sterlyn Group, and the three steps that follow

This is the part I am closest to right now. Wooden Boy Industries brings the AI strategy, the product thinking, the workflow implementation, and the staff enablement. Mark Clausman and Sterlyn Group bring deep healthcare IT, information security, HIPAA, and operational technology experience. Put those together and you can move an organization faster while keeping trust, compliance, and human judgment in the design from day one, instead of bolted on after something goes wrong.

The path we run has three steps, and they are deliberately small before they are big. An AI Opportunity Scan looks across leadership, operations, care navigation, quality, compliance, and support, ranks the opportunities by value and risk, and selects the use cases worth pursuing. A Guardrail Sprint sets the data-use boundaries so adoption is safe instead of hopeful. Pilot to Practice takes one or two of those use cases and turns them into a real 30 to 60 day implementation plan with goals you can measure.

The point of the sequence is restraint. You do not need forty AI projects. You need two or three high-value workflows, built where they reduce friction without creating new risk, and a plan that an actual person can run on Monday.

One number worth keeping

MIT's NANDA initiative studied this directly in its 2025 report, The GenAI Divide, drawing on 150 leader interviews, a survey of 350 employees, and 300 public AI deployments. The finding: about 95 percent of enterprise generative AI pilots delivered no measurable impact on the bottom line. Only around 5 percent broke through. The same research found that buying from specialized vendors and building partnerships worked about 67 percent of the time, while going it alone internally succeeded roughly a third as often. Read those two numbers next to each other and the lesson is plain. The pilots that stall are not stalling because the technology is weak, they stall because nobody did the governance and implementation work, and because the organization tried to build it alone instead of bringing in people who had done it before.

Beyond the Chat Box

There is a quieter job underneath all of this, and it is the one I would protect first: deciding what is worth doing at all. Most of what crosses an executive's desk about AI right now is vendor noise and novelty. A real implementation partner spends as much energy saying no to the shiny distraction as saying yes to the useful pilot.

That is also the work that survives a reorg. Plenty of organizations are rebuilding their IT, modernizing old systems, or bracing staff for a wave of new expectations. In that environment, change management is not a soft add-on, it is the thing that decides whether any of this sticks. The tools will keep changing. The discipline of choosing the right few, putting boundaries around them, and helping people trust them is what holds.

The Question

Two questions to carry into your next leadership meeting, because they sort the real work from the noise. Which one workflow in your organization would change the most if AI were built into it correctly, with the data boundaries handled? And what is the one place where, no matter how good the tool gets, a human has to stay accountable for the call?

The first question is where you start. The second is where you draw the line. A good implementation respects both, and so should anyone you let near your data.
